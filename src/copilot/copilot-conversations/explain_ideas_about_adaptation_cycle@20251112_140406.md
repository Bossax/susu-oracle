It is viewed not as a series of standalone activities but as a continuous, integrated national cycle that links actions at the project, sectoral, and national levels [^1].

The cycle generally consists of four main stages, with some models adding a foundational stage [^2, ^3].

### Stages of the Adaptation Cycle

1.  **Climate Science Foundation (in some models)**
    This initial stage focuses on establishing the scientific basis for adaptation efforts [^3].

2.  **Impact, Vulnerability, and Risk Assessment (IVRA)**
    This stage involves conducting up-to-date assessments of climate hazards, impacts, and exposure to risks and vulnerabilities [^2]. The concept of risk is central, defined as the potential for adverse consequences for human and ecological systems, emphasizing negative impacts [^4]. These assessments are crucial for informing the development of national adaptation plans and strategies [^2]. A key target is for all parties to establish multi-hazard early warning systems and climate information services [^2].

3.  **Planning**
    In this stage, the insights from the IVRA are used to create adaptation portfolios and formulate country-driven national adaptation plans (NAPs) and policies [^2, ^3]. These plans should be gender-responsive, participatory, and transparent, and adaptation should be mainstreamed into all relevant national strategies [^2].

4.  **Implementation**
    This stage involves financing and executing the adaptation portfolios and plans developed in the previous stage [^2, ^3]. The primary goal is to progress in implementing these plans to reduce the social and economic impacts of identified climate hazards [^2].

5.  **Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL)**
    The final stage is to design and operationalize a system for monitoring, evaluating, and learning from national adaptation efforts [^2]. This involves building the institutional capacity to fully implement the system [^2]. At the project level, good MEL practice includes developing a performance monitoring plan that specifies how, when, and by whom data will be collected [^5].
